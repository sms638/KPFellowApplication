Kleiner Perkins Fellowship Application

What impact do you want to have on the world and why?

Technology has the power to be an equalizer. However, most technology is made with one type of user in mind: someone without disabilities. My long-term goal is to engineer technology solutions for social good with a focus on accessibility, usability, and equitability. Throughout high school, I conducted research at Drexel University’s Human Computer Interaction Lab on the use of technology for children with autism. I worked closely with a professor and postdoc students to research and jointly publish on the usability of apps. The findings from the research highlighted a disparity: while technology can have positive effects on children’s special education, there is a massive disconnect between those creating the technology, and the actual users. Technology can be used to mitigate developmental issues, but a myriad of users need to be taken into account to ensure applications are accessible. Currently I am enrolled in a UI/UX class to further study accessibility and equitable design in software engineering. My passion for user-friendly technology led me to intern at Thinker-Tinker, a start-up that creates tech toys to help children learn. Working on features for their upcoming mobile app in a fast-paced environment fueled my desire to become an entrepreneur and one day start my own company with social good in mind. The mentorship and community I would experience as a Kleiner Perkins fellow would provide me with the foundation to engineer innovative technologies that benefit a greater population.

Describe your most meaningful experience(s) and why they matter to you.

In middle school, my first exposure to computer science was through a coding summer camp. I enjoyed the curriculum but attended begrudgingly after realizing I was the only girl in the entire 40 person camp. I felt very alone and thought perhaps computer science was too “nerdy”. This initial experience sparked my desire to see gender equality in technology.  What if I could use my entrepreneurial spirit to create a change? Growing up, I vividly remember trying to “sell” my sister my latest lego creations and designing and printing new stickers to trade when sticker collecting was all the rage. But as I’ve gotten older, I’ve realized that I could harness these same entrepreneurial instincts to create something with a larger impact.

I turned to securing a grant from NCWIT Aspire IT and finding a school to partner with in order to create a coding summer camp for girls. Every summer throughout high school, I worked with the Overbrook Educational Center (OEC), a title I Philadelphia school. I taught a free one-week coding camp for their middle school girls. OEC did not have a technology curriculum, so this camp was the students’ first exposure to computer science. Each year I organized women technology speakers, and designed and taught a new curriculum. I created lessons on Scratch, HTML, and virtual reality.  Close bonds formed as the same students came back to attend the camp summer after summer. It was rewarding to know that I had successfully shared the allure of programming.
